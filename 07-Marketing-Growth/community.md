## What "community" means here

Community is not a feature. It's a relationship with the people who care about what we care about. For Culture Wiki, community is:

- Readers who come back.
- Correctors who fix errors.
- Claimers who verify their own profiles.
- Writers who want to contribute.
- Artists, producers, journalists who trust us enough to be sources.

We build community to be useful to us *and* useful to them. Anything that isn't mutual is extraction — we don't do that.

## Community tiers

### Tier 1 — Readers
Anyone who visits the site. No account required. We serve them through the product itself.

**What we owe them:**
- Fast, accurate, comprehensive pages.
- Mobile-excellent experience.
- Their history respected (no forced account creation).

### Tier 2 — Registered users
Users who created an account. Can favorite, create lists, submit corrections.

**What we owe them:**
- Never spam.
- Clear value for registration (favorites, lists, correction credit).
- Respect for their data (see `08-Legal-Compliance/privacy-policy-draft.md`).

**What they owe us:**
- Honest interactions.
- Compliance with terms.

### Tier 3 — Contributors
Users who actively add value: correctors, researchers, eventual writers.

**What we offer them:**
- Public credit (optional, their choice).
- Access to contributor-only channels (Discord, internal docs).
- First-to-know on new features.
- Paid work when budget allows.

**How they become contributors:**
- Submit 5+ accepted corrections → invited to contributor tier.
- Pitch editorial piece and deliver → paid contributor.
- Demonstrate expertise in a region or era → regional editor candidate.

### Tier 4 — Subjects (artists, producers, journalists)
The people the archive documents.

**What we offer them:**
- Free profile forever.
- Control over self-identification fields.
- Right to correct facts.
- Visibility to audience who cares about them.

**What we never offer them:**
- Paid preferential placement.
- Editorial exemption from critique.
- Veto over sourced facts.

## Community venues

### On-site (MVP)
- User profiles (basic: display name, bio, lists).
- Public lists (shared URLs).
- Correction submissions (our primary contribution surface).

### Off-site

#### Newsletter (MVP)
- Weekly.
- Mix: new entries, editorial of the week, one cultural note.
- Not a promo channel.
- Unsubscribe always one click away.
- Target by end of Year 1: 5,000 subscribers.

#### Social accounts (MVP)
- Twitter/X, Instagram primary. Voice per `04-Editorial/style-guide.md`.
- Not acquisition-focused. More like signal-of-existence.

#### Discord (Phase B)
- Niche server for power users, contributors, discussion of the scene.
- Light moderation.
- Not scaled for broad audience. Quality > quantity.
- 3-5 channels:
  - #general
  - #new-entries
  - #corrections-and-research
  - #editorial-writers (gated)
  - #announcements (one-way, editor-only)

#### Email inbox (MVP)
- hello@... for general inquiries.
- corrections@... for moderation (could be handled in-app, but email for those who prefer).
- press@... for press inquiries.
- legal@... for legal matters.

## Engagement strategy (not manipulation)

We do not use dark patterns or engagement-maximization tactics. No streaks, no badges, no addictive loops.

What we do:

- **Notify selectively.** Email notifications only when genuinely useful (correction accepted, weekly newsletter, artist you follow has a new release we're tracking).
- **Credit visibly.** When users contribute, we show it (optional for the user).
- **Build rituals.** A weekly newsletter, a predictable publish day, a clear monthly editorial calendar create rhythm. Rituals beat notifications.
- **Ask for input.** Periodic user surveys. Open suggestion form in footer.
- **Respond personally.** For the first year at least, Luis responds to every substantive email / message. Scale with a team as it grows.

## Artist relationship strategy

Artists are the most important relationship category. A good relationship with the artist community is:

- A moat (competitors can't manufacture it).
- A source of truth (corrections from artists > corrections from fans).
- A social amplifier (artists share their own profile when they approve of it).

### How we build that relationship

**At ingestion time:**
- We don't contact artists proactively. We build the profile using public sources.
- When the profile is live and quality is high, we send a one-time respectful message: "Your profile is live. Here's the link. If you want to claim and verify, here's how. Free, no strings."

**After claim:**
- Claimed artists get edit capabilities on specific fields (photo, bio, social handles) with light review.
- Their corrections on factual matters still need sources but get priority review.
- They can submit new career events, new releases, new photos.

**In conflicts:**
- Artist says "remove that" on something we have sourced from press → we keep it but discuss the framing.
- Artist says "change my name to X" → we check public branding; accept if consistent; discuss if not.
- Artist says "remove this whole profile" → we evaluate public interest. Active career generally means we keep but may adjust framing. Retired artist's wish is generally honored.

### What we never do

- Charge for profile creation or improvement.
- Charge to remove negative information.
- Remove sourced facts on request without a better source.
- Accept gifts, favors, or back-channel influence.
- Treat unsigned / emerging artists worse than established ones.

## Correction culture

Corrections are not a complaint channel — they're a contribution channel. Our culture around them:

- **Thank every accepted correction personally** (automated email with a human-written template).
- **Credit publicly if the user opts in** ("Profile updated with information from: [username]" in edit log).
- **Track top correctors.** Top 10 monthly invited to contributor tier.
- **Never shame users for rejected corrections.** Rejection emails are kind and explain what would have worked.

### The correction feedback loop

Good correctors become good contributors become good editors. We actively cultivate this pipeline:

1. User submits 1-2 corrections. We accept.
2. We invite them to #corrections-and-research on Discord.
3. If they sustain quality, we offer them a small research project.
4. If that goes well, we offer them editorial pitches.
5. Eventually, maybe they're on staff.

Not everyone follows this arc. But the ladder exists for those who want it.

## Safety and moderation

Covered in `06-Operations/moderation-workflow.md`. Community-specific additions:

- **Harassment of other users.** Immediate warning, second offense = suspension.
- **Coordinated attacks on artists.** Takedown + investigation.
- **Bot accounts.** Detected and banned aggressively.
- **Impersonation** (pretending to be an artist). Immediate suspension + verification check.

## Editorial community (contributor relationships)

As editorial scales, we manage contributor relationships carefully.

### Onboarding flow for writers
1. Informal reach-out (either they pitched us or we invited them based on their published work).
2. One-on-one call to align on voice, topics, expectations.
3. First assignment: small, low-stakes, tight editorial feedback.
4. Second assignment: ambitious. If it lands, they're in.
5. Ongoing: recurring pitches, fair pay, credit.

### Contributor expectations document
Every contributor gets a short doc with:
- Payment terms.
- Editorial rights.
- Publication timeline.
- Attribution norms.
- Conflict-of-interest policies (contributor cannot write about artist they manage or are related to).

### Building a contributor bench
Target: 3-5 active contributors by end of Year 1. 10+ by end of Year 2.

- Source: people who emerged through corrections, people we admire from their own platforms, people who pitch us unsolicited.
- Mix: geographic (different cities / countries as we expand), generational (older voices for historical eras, younger voices for current scenes), perspective (journalists, academics, artists-turned-writers).

## Scene relationships

Beyond individual artists, we engage with the hip-hop scene as a whole:

- Show up (digitally and physically when possible) at scene events.
- Support other independent Spanish-language music outlets — cross-promote when aligned.
- Don't pick fights with competitors or other outlets. We're building an ecosystem, not a war.
- Host or sponsor small events when budget allows: meetups, panels at existing festivals, editorial roundtables.

## Metrics

### MVP-era
- Registered users.
- Correction volume.
- Correction acceptance rate.
- Newsletter subscribers and open rate.
- Return visitor rate.

### Growth-era
- Discord members.
- Claimed artist profiles.
- Top correctors list.
- Contributor count.
- User engagement scores (lists created, favorites added).

### Long-term
- Ratio of archive-active users (visit + interact) to passive readers.
- Time-to-contributor (how long from first visit to first contribution).
- Community health: no toxic incidents, low ban rate, sustained correction volume.
