# Vision

**Department:** Executive
**Owner:** Luis Figuera
**Last updated:** April 22, 2026

---

## The problem

Spanish-language rap has 40+ years of history, a continental footprint, and produces some of the most innovative music in the global hip-hop landscape today. Venezuelan rap specifically has a rich, interconnected scene that has influenced artists across the hispanic world.

Yet none of this is structured, citable, or discoverable in a serious way.

The information exists, but it is fragmented:

- **Streaming platforms** (Spotify, Apple) have discographies but no context, no lineage, no story.
- **Wikipedia** in Spanish is thin, with many stub articles or outright missing pages for central figures.
- **Genius** has lyrics but almost no producer credits, label history, or career context for Latin artists.
- **Podcasts and YouTube interviews** have deep knowledge trapped in hours of audio, impossible to search or cross-reference.
- **Press coverage** is scattered across dozens of outlets in different languages and formats.
- **Oral history** — the deepest source — lives in the memory of artists, producers, DJs, and journalists, and is being lost as the scene ages.

Meanwhile, the English-language hip-hop scene has Genius, Discogs, AllMusic, DJBooth, Complex, Pitchfork, and dozens of academic archives. There is no equivalent infrastructure for Spanish rap. This is a cultural gap, and it is a business opportunity.

## The opportunity

Spanish-language music has been the fastest-growing category in global streaming for three consecutive years. Hip-hop specifically is underserved on the data and editorial side while over-represented on the consumption side. The audience exists. The artists exist. The stories exist. The connective tissue — the archive that links it all — does not.

Culture Wiki is that connective tissue.

## The vision

Within five years, Culture Wiki is the canonical reference for Spanish-language hip-hop — cited by Billboard, Rolling Stone en Español, Remezcla, and academic institutions studying Latin American popular culture. It is the first place a journalist, manager, label A&R, fan, or researcher goes when they want to understand an artist, a scene, a relationship, or a historical moment.

It is **independent** — not owned by a label, platform, or media conglomerate.
It is **data-rich** — every fact sourced, every entity connected to every other.
It is **editorially serious** — original writing that contextualizes the archive.
It is **community-aware** — artists verify their own profiles, fans contribute corrections, the scene recognizes itself in it.

## What it is not

- Not a streaming platform. We don't host music.
- Not a social network. We don't optimize for engagement at the cost of truth.
- Not a fan blog. We don't speculate, we don't rank for clicks, we don't sensationalize.
- Not a label, manager, or promoter. We don't represent artists commercially.
- Not dependent on any single source or voice.

## Why now

Four forces converge:

1. **Latin music is ascendant.** Streaming growth is continuous, institutional attention is increasing.
2. **Documentary-style music content is booming.** Netflix, Spotify Studios, PBS, and independents have proven audience appetite for music history done seriously.
3. **AI enables the impossible.** Five years ago, structuring the information trapped in thousands of hours of video interviews, scattered articles, and oral history would have required a team of 20 researchers for a decade. Today, one engineer with the right tools and editorial direction can do it in months.
4. **The scene's pioneers are aging.** The founders of Venezuelan rap are in their 40s and 50s. If the archive isn't built now, it loses primary sources permanently.

## Why Venezuelan-first

Venezuelan rap is an ideal seed because:

- It has **extraordinary depth** relative to its size. Multiple generations, regional diversity, stylistic range.
- It has **pan-Hispanic influence** that lets us expand naturally. Venezuelan artists collaborate with Mexicans, Colombians, Spaniards, Argentinians, Puerto Ricans — so documenting Venezuela naturally pulls the rest of the scene into the graph.
- It has **a diaspora** that amplifies reach (NYC, Madrid, Miami, Bogotá, Buenos Aires, Mexico City).
- It is **underdocumented** to an extreme degree, which means the marginal value of every entry is high.
- It is **emotionally resonant** for the founder — authenticity compounds.

The foothold is Venezuelan. The platform is pan-Hispanic.

## Core beliefs

- **Data without story is a spreadsheet. Story without data is folklore. We do both.**
- **The graph is the product.** Pages are just views into it.
- **Completeness compounds.** Every new entity enriches every previous one.
- **Authority is earned, not claimed.** Sourcing everything is not overhead — it is the moat.
- **Scale through structure.** Hand-crafted pages don't scale. Templates driven by a shared data model do.

## Five-year horizon

**Year 1:** MVP with Venezuelan core. 200+ artists, 2,000+ releases, 10,000+ entity connections. Audience of Spanish-speaking hip-hop fans worldwide. First revenue from sponsorships and memberships.

**Year 2:** Pan-Hispanic expansion. Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Spain, Puerto Rico, Chile. 1,000+ artists. Editorial staff of 2-3 contributors. Recognized by at least one major Latin music publication as a source.

**Year 3:** Data licensing to institutions. Dataset becomes a commercial asset. Academic citations begin. Platform is self-sustaining.

**Year 4:** Multi-language (English edition for international press and diaspora). Video content. Live events.

**Year 5:** The reference. Culturally and commercially.
